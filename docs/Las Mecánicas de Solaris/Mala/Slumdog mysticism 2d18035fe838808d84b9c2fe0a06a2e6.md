# Slumdog mysticism

Mala and Menta squatted next to each other in the back alley of the tavern. Despite the gravity of the topic at hand, Mala seemed more annoyed than afraid, and Menta seemed more excited than worried.

“So that’s what corruption is? Am I corrupted?”, said Mala.

“Yes, and yes. But don’t worry, I can fix it.”

“This the part where you say ‘for a price’?”

“What? No! The taste of corruption itself is the payment!”

*Isn’t that perilous? Consuming numens beyond our mortal comprehension?*

“That good for your body?”, said Mala.

“Maybe? I don’t know. What I know is that it feels good.”

That’s when Mala knew she’d found a kindred soul.

---

The two girls made their way towards the inn. It had already turned late enough that Brasa was high in the sky.

Menta seemed unsure of many things. Her supposed mastery as a “mystic” didn’t hold up to Mala’s scrutiny, but Menta insisted she knew what she was doing. Most of Mala’s questions were met with maybes and don’t knows. She began to suspect something was afoot.

“I have a question”, said Mala.

“Yes?”

“Are you a child predator?”

Menta burst out laughing.

“Don’t be silly! Besides, I am barely out of childhood, myself.”

“Why you giving me the runaround with all these questions, then?”

“Because I genuinely don’t know!” Menta threw her hands up. “Look, I am a specialist in corruption, not in unprecedented astral alignments. If I am not mistaken, it is the first time such a thing happens. Why would I have all the answers? Ask a scholar, not me.”

It made sense. Kinda. Mala still wasn’t sure she wouldn’t be butchered in her sleep.

“So, about the corruption…”, asked Mala.

“Yes, yes. The black goo is everyone’s first contact with deep corruption.” Menta bent over to get on Mala’s eye level. “Look, I don’t want to sound prejudiced, but Expiados like you are susceptible to corruption.” Menta straightened her back again. “Everyone went mad, it’s just… You went worse.”

“You telling me this happened to Expiados all over the world?”

“No, no. I am sure you had your circumstances, like not being close to firelight, or…”

“I slept by a bonfire.”

Menta stopped in her tracks.

“Oh, then… that’s *no bueno*.”

---

A thick smoke with a fresh herbal smell came from a censer in the middle of Menta’s rented room. Her open-toed boots lay discarded by the entrance, and so would Mala’s if she had any.

“It’s almost time for the short mooncycle siesta; the ritual has to be done during the suncycle”, mentioned Menta as she dug for something in her travel bag. “You can crash here if you want.”

“Eh, I don’t know…”, replied Mala from the edge of the only bed in sight.

“Come on”, Menta scoffed, tossing a pillow at Mala. “Who in their right mind would kick a child out?”

“My caravaneer.”

Menta sighed.

“Do you need me to lend you some pajamas?” Menta asked.

“You use pajamas? Ain’t that for kids?”

Menta stared at Mala with tensed lips. Not quite a smile, not quite a frown.

“Fine”, said Mala, dragging out each syllable. “But only 'cuz my clothes are full of shit.”

“… literally?”

“Am not sure.”

Menta’s pajamas did not fit Mala. They were several sizes too big, especially around the chest, and they made her look more like a mop than a kid ready to sleep. And yet, they had that soothing, refreshing aroma that followed Menta everywhere she went.

“What’s this smell?”, asked Mala.

“Mint. My special blend. Do you like it?”

Mala gathered around her nose the flaps of clothes dangling from her arms. She closed her eyes and inhaled until her lungs were full. It was the first time she immersed herself in this new aroma. Back in her hometown, Lomasa, this plant did not exist, but what existed were the comforting shadows of the half cave it was built in, the rivers and cascades of cool water, and the occasional snow of the nearby peaks. The smell of mint reminded her of that.

In a way, it felt like home.

It wasn’t long before Mala and Menta lay side by side in the queen-sized bed. Mala wondered why Menta would need to rent a room clearly meant for a couple when individual rooms were much cheaper. Mala looked to her side: Menta already had her eyes closed, trying to fall asleep.

“Hey”, whispered Mala.

“Yes?”, replied Menta in a raspy voice, eyes still closed.

“You sure you are not a child predator?”

A lazy chuckle escaped Menta’s lips. She patted Mala’s hair.

“Sleep well, kid”, whispered Menta.

Mala tried. Oh, did she try! Alas, the ceiling proved more interesting than anything she found behind her eyelids. Menta was already snoring softly, her forked tongue lolling from her mouth, but it wasn’t the noise keeping Mala awake.

The girl just felt… off. There was nothing wrong with her in specific, but she wasn’t fine either. A bit too much sweat, a vague sense of dizziness in her head, unease. At first, she thought the incense might have been laced with something, but then she realized it was not the first time she had experienced these symptoms.

She hadn’t felt this particular brand of misery since she ran out of money for the first time, a few months ago.

High mooncycle of twin moons, eight shots of ratafía in. The caravan she was traveling in back then stopped by a town called Limbo. It was a paradise in the middle of the desert: large, built around an oasis with springs, plenty of water, wine, and money.

The casino was the first place Mala visited.

Fountains, golden arches, immaculate white columns, tiny lighthouses on top… that place had it all. It felt more like a palace than a temple of decadence; it was part of the setup: no one wanted to appear out of place in such an opulent place, no one wanted to be seen as the “poor” gambler, so they all spent more than they could afford.

Mala was used to playing dice, cards, and the like, but this place was different: they had imported some mechanisms from the casino in Libertaria, and that was a paradigm change in the field of gambling. One of these machines captivated the girl’s attention like nothing else before: the estraperlo.

The estraperlo was a machine that consisted of an ornate spinning wheel with numbered slots surrounding its perimeter. A spiral rail curved inwards from the edge to the center. When a gambler threw a marble into the ever-spinning wheel, it would follow the spiral rail toward the middle, then bounce outward to fall into one of the slots in the perimeter of the machine. If you guessed the number of the lucky slot, or whether it would be even or odd, you got a payout. Finally, the casino operator had to pull a lever to release the ball back into the table. It’s obvious the lever trick was more for theatrics than practicality; extracting the ball by hand would have been more cost-effective.

At first, Mala’s plays were wonderful. She began testing the mechanism by making small bets on the marble hitting odd numbers until she was convinced she had nailed down her throwing technique. Then, she applied her tried and tested strategy of doubling her bet on every failure to recover the losses.

Mala doubled, tripled her money. The entire table was cheering her on, until even the waiters began treating her to more and more shots of increasingly exotic liquors.

She got too cocky.

As easy as the gains came, so did they go. Improbable loss after improbable loss forced Mala to double down on every bet until the amount she had to bet to recover became too big to afford. She went all in.

Zero coins.

Fueled in part by her alcoholic bravado, Mala jumped on top of her stool, ready to jump onto the table and stomp on it, but she was grabbed by the surrounding staff.

*Twelve losses in a row? This is a statistical mockery!*

“Unfair! Unfair, unfair, unfair!”, she shouted at the top of her lungs.

Somehow, Mala managed to wrestle away the people holding her back, and, in a fit of rage, she ripped apart the cloth that covered the underside of the estraperlo’s table.

That’s when she, and everyone else, saw the pedals: dirty wooden and leather bellows tucked right under where the operator had been standing.

Needless to say, no one was happy.

Mala took advantage of the ensuing brawl to sneak under the table and examine the architect of her misery from closer up. The bellows appeared to be connected through pneumatic tubes to the underside of the wheel. She pulled the cover plate off and found an air bag tucked right under the slots. Of course the marble always bounced away!

She poked around the intricate mechanisms, absorbed by their craftsmanship. She was so captivated by the fancy lever mechanism that not even the operator’s body slamming against the surface of the table startled her. She could have stayed there for hours, had she not been pulled by her tail by a calloused hand.

Mala soon found herself dangling down by her tail, staring at a man in a *lorica musculata*.

“Let me down, you imbecile!”

“You’re coming with me, little miss.”

Mala was thrown without ceremony into the dungeons of the guardhouse. She shouted and cried and begged to be let out, without success.

The worst part of prison was the lack of alcohol. Sure, they brought her water, canapés from the casino leftovers, books, and a guard even brought her cute toys to play with, but all she wanted was a drink. A cold, strong one. She was denied the only thing she truly needed.

It felt like an eternity passed behind those bars.

By the second mooncycle, however, things took a turn for the worse. Sweat, fever, insomnia, anxiety; the same symptoms she was experiencing during her mooncycle in Bodega. She’d decided to shrug it off, since the local sheriff had promised to release her early in the suncycle.

“Well, miss, technically, you have done nothing illegal.”

“Can I get my money back?”

The sheriff just laughed.

Back then, she’d attributed those odd symptoms to a cold, even if they lasted for too short a time.

But now she put two and two together.

“Shiiiiiit.”

Mala jumped off the bed, caring little about disturbing Menta’s sleep, and began rummaging through Menta’s bag with shaky, desperate hands. Clothes, makeup, herbs and incense, an odd-looking carved stick, a heavy leather-bound ledger, fortune-telling cards, essential oils… and what she was looking for. Bingo.

The little Expiada produced an adorned bottle half full of a green liquid from the travel bag. It looked expensive: patterned glass cut like a precious gem, a golden cap, and a metal plate with an intricate engraving of a bouquet of flowers; however, judging by the wear and tear of the glass and cap, and the rust on the plate, this particular bottle had seen more than one refill.

Mala wasted no time in opening the bottle and drinking its contents. The strong mint flavor stung her tongue, and the pure alcohol burnt her throat like liquid fire, but to her, this was the most comforting sensation she’d felt in many days.

A tiny fresh-scented burp marked the satisfactory end of Mala’s indulgence.

*Now, how do I entertain myself during my pre-ebriety interval?*

Mala considered for a moment, trying out Menta’s makeup, or hiding under the bed, imagining she was exploring a dark cavern, but that would be too childish. Instead, Mala decided on playing a practical prank on her new companion.

The little girl kneeled in front of the only door in the room and began pulling at one of the floorboards. The inn was an old construction, so the wood came off easily with her trusty knife and a bit of Menta’s essential oils.

The plan was simple: turn the floorboard into a seesaw, put some makeup powder on the end facing the door, and wait for Menta to step on it whenever she attempted to go out of the room. Menta would step on the microscopic catapult, launching the makeup powders into the air, which would paint her green whole!

Mala started giggling like a maniac. Of course, it made more sense in her head, even if anyone could tell it was closer to a tripping hazard than anything else.

The girl balanced the floorboard around the middle using the rusty nails that were holding the floorboard in place. After some tweaking, the trap was perfectly leveled and at the same height as the other boards. Almost imperceptible.

It was when Mala was about to gather Menta’s makeup that her large, pointy ears picked up the footsteps of someone approaching the door. Mala dropped the makeup bag and ran to her side of the bed, then sneaked on top to pretend she was sleeping as well.

There was a knock on the door.

“Menta, open the door”, a muffled, deep male voice came from the other side of the door. “We need to talk.”

Menta did not wake up. Heavy sleeper.

The door's handle clicked, but it was locked. The man knocked again, harder.

“Menta! I know you are here! Open the door!”

Menta opened her eyes. Her slit pupils contracted as they adjusted to the residual light of the twilight. She pushed herself upright, still groggy from her deep sleep, and adjusted her green nightgown’s straps.

“Look, Menta, my… the villagers”, continued the man. “They made me come here, in the middle of the night, and I’d much rather be back at home sleeping than doing this, so if you could kindly open the door…”

Menta tapped Mala, who was still pretending to sleep, on the shoulder.

“Ah… what is it?”, asked Mala, pretending to wake up.

“Look, I don’t want to scare you”, whispered Menta into her ears, “but the sheriff is here, and you should pack up your things and…”

Another knock, this time much more violent, made the room tremble.

“Menta! Open. The. Door!”, shouted the man.

The man did not wait. He started attempting to batter down the door.

Boom.

The two girls grabbed their bags.

Boom.

Menta pulled away the window’s heavy curtains and wooden blinds.

Boom!

The door flew open with a deafening crack, wooden splinters scattering into the air.

A hulking Lenamite man stood in the cracked frame of the door. He wore a military uniform, an oversized gladius dangling from his hip, a lorica segmentata, and a prominent mustache that covered the lower half of his face. His eyes were bloodshot, his skin red with fury and slick with sweat. For a moment, Mala and Menta feared for their lives.

The sheriff lunged towards them, each step a ground-shaking stomp. He’d only taken two steps before he stepped on the rigged floorboard.

Blam! The board broke in half, sending one end straight into his face. The man flinched.

Menta took advantage of the situation to smash her travel bag against the window, shattering the glass pane. She threw the bag to the street below to cushion their fall.

“Mala! Jump!”, shouted Menta.

Mala hopped on the window frame and looked back. For a moment, she saw the panicked face of Menta, but the short mooncycle had already started to wane, and the first bright rays of sun illuminated the face of the sheriff well for the first time.

In one of his cheeks, he had the mark of a wide-open palm. On the other, the faded traces of green lipstick.

Mala jumped, and behind her, so did Menta.

And they ran.

Halfway through Bodega, edge of the town in sight, the absurdity of the whole situation hit Mala: she came to Bodega to get some booze, run a few odd jobs and jump onto the next caravan, so why was she following a crazy fugitive woman who took a suspicious liking to her?

"Menta! Why me?!", shouted Mala as both crossed the city outskirts of Bodega.

"What?! Just run!"

"You don't even know my name...". Mala gasped for air. "Why help me?"

"I told you!", Menta panted. "Your corruption!"

"No, it's 'cuz you pity me! You lie!"

Mala shot Menta a doubtful glare, only to find out Menta returning a more desperate one. They looked back in unison: no sheriff in sight. Both slowed to a halt.

"You're full of it!", shouted Mala, then filled her lungs for what she was about to say. "You're full of it and through! You don't know shit about corruption! Your cologne bottle has been refilled a million times! The harlot makeup...!"

Mala pointed an accusing finger at Menta. Menta jumped back, freezing at the accusation, flinching at the word "harlot".

"You're a *ratera* from *la calle*, just like me!"

Mala's ears twitched and turned back. Accelerated footsteps... no, stomps. The sheriff was closing in on them. Mala grabbed Menta's hand and dragged her into a run.

"I like that!", said Mala.

That’s when Menta knew she’d found a kindred soul.
