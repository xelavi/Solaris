# Unnamed Celestial Event

"*¡Adiós, mama!* Mala's off to become the bestest pioneer!"

"Okay, honey! Pack a little something warm!"

One year later, Mala lay flattened face down against a boulder, skin fused to stone, enduring the unbearable heat of the dry season. Had she any moisture left inside of her, she would have oozed sweat right now, but it'd been so long since she drank anything that the insides of her mouth had already turned to sandpaper.

*Well, this is quite the conundrum*, she thought to herself.

"Balls", escaped her mouth.

Mala was supposed to be travelling with a caravan to Libertaria, but the last coins in her pocket forced her to choose: one last refill of her flask, or paying the caravaneer's fee. Mala, ever the calculating gambler, chose the obvious answer. After all, who in their right mind would abandon a small, innocent child in the middle of a steppe?

Unfortunately for Mala, blowing her money on booze doesn't seem to be anyone's definition of innocent.

Despite it all, even if her tail drooped and her ears flopped, the little Expiada's spirit remained unbroken. So what if she gambled away all of her money again? She would gamble it all back, and then she would go on to lead the grandest expedition into the high seas that Solmen had ever witnessed, and then she would be rich and famous and could drink and smoke all she wanted. All she had to do was to survive the next mooncycle.

Mala unglued her face off the boulder, head still sizzling a bit, and rummaged through the contents of her backpack: clothes, a travel diary (blank), an empty coin pouch, a knife, a map of the region, and the vague memory of having pawned everything else. Not great, but she could eat her clothes in a pinch. Mala unfolded the map and started paying attention to it for the very first time in her entire quest. Loose lines, shaky borders, and yet the cartographer swore on his mother that the landmarks were in the right place. Her caravan crossed a derelict brick bridge two suncycles ago, so that should put her anywhere between the icon of a crumbling bridge and the huge triangle representing Mount Pharos, already visible in the distance.

*What would be the optimal policy for this biome?*, Mala thought as her eyes scanned the map. She was no scholar, nor well educated, nor... well, it was a miracle she knew how to read, and barely so, but that never stopped her from trying. And it was not like it was her first time surviving in the wilderness! She had some experience camping behind her house, and that must count for something.

As Mala studied the scroll to pinpoint her location, the shadows of her own hat blurred over the scribbles in the map. Brasa and Cendra, the twin moons were already looming high. Two moons were a bad omen: the mooncycle would be longer and darker than usual, and with it, emboldened predators. It would also provide some relief from the face-melting heat, but that just meant trading heatstroke for hypothermia.

Her mind was already racing with the checklist of things to do: water, shelter, something to get a buzz from, snacks. In that order.

Now, Mala may not have known what a map scale was, or whether such a thing would be reliable with such lousy cartography, but she knew the caravan took a week to go from Peñasco to La Huerta, and that meant the four or so thumbs separating them in the map could extrapolate one week of travel to any other point. More or less.

After many minutes of the pinnacle of mathematics that a semi-illiterate mind could process, Mala arrived at the conclusion that she had to be close to a large lake next to a crag.

"South", she mumbled. "Ish".

---

Mala was right, more by chance than by accurate calculations. After two hours of walking, she found the lake. Judging by the extension of "beach" the body of water had, she guessed it had to be much bigger during the wet season. Right now, however, it was little more than a pond. *Enough to satisfy my needs*. Mala filled her metal flask and moved on: she knew better than drinking from still water.

Next on the list, shelter. Not even Mala's cocky mind could pretend there was time to build a refuge, let alone a refuge that held on its own, so she had to settle for whatever kind of hole the land could provide. The crag was a decent starting point, and with a bit of luck, she could find a crevice in the rock to pass the night camouflaged from the creatures outside. If the crevice was narrow enough, maybe it could even retain the heat from the mandatory bonfire.

Oh, right. The bonfire.

The holy teachings of the Maker of the Imperishable Fire were clear: fire protects. From the cold, from unwanted presences... from fear. When things get darker and shadows mantle the world, the mind becomes your enemy. For her own sanity (and because she was just a kid, of course she would be afraid of the dusk), Mala knew she had to secure a bonfire, if only to boil the water in her flask.

To start a fire, you need three simple things: a light scrap to kickstart the flame, a body of flammable fuel, and a tool to ignite it all. And she had none of that.

Only a fool would be unable to find timber in a steppe in the middle of the dry season. She was surrounded by dry grass in every direction, so that would not be an issue. Now, for the other two requirements...

Mala checked the pockets of her backpack again, hoping she'd missed something. She found a matchbox. Her face lit up as that of a child unwrapping presents on their birthday, but her happiness was short-lived: the box was empty. Of course, the one time one of her vices could save her life instead of shortening her lifespan, it flaked: smoking was her least favorite vice, and it was the first to go when money started lacking.

The girl looked up, and found the moons looming over her. *I must proceed posthaste*, she pondered, *lest I become any scavenger's supper*. She regretted thinking about supper.

If her calculations were correct, she still had two or three hours of bright time; any longer, and the deep dusk would make it impossible for her to identify potential threats, let alone useful materials. Food and getting high would have to wait until tomorrow.

Without a clear idea of how she would even light the bonfire, Mala set off to find anything to burn.

You see, the steppe isn't particularly bountiful when it comes to firewood. There was grass everywhere the eye could see, there were shrubs that could provide twigs... but bushes or trees are scarce, and even if they weren't, her best shot at even attempting to gather from them was to smash them with rocks until they fell. Mala considered for a moment burning the clothes in her backpack, but then she remembered those were her emergency rations.

There was one way out, though. A nasty one.

She sighed, resigned. Mala knelt down next to a large pile of herbivore turds, pulled a hood from her backpack to use as a bag, unwrapped it on the ground, and began picking the balls one by one with the grace of a princess choosing her earrings for the day. It was, without a doubt, the most humiliating time of her life.

Carrying a bag full of excrement on her shoulder, she backtracked her way to a place she spotted in the perimeter of the crag. It wasn't quite a cave, that would be too charitable, more like a crack in the stone that was wide enough to house her, and not very well at that. Enough for this mooncycle, at least.

Mala dumped the dung bag, hood and all, on top of the pile of dry grass and twigs she had been feeding. If she rationed it, it seemed enough to last early into the suncycle. Mala wiped her hand on the rock, hard enough to bring a blush to her fingertips. Now, there was only the matter of finding a spark.

As Mala dug through her backpack for the third time this suncycle, the light fading to orange gave her one last warning: twilight was imminent. She had minutes to figure a solution before the mooncycle descended.

Again, the same items came out of the pack. Clothes, diary, pouch, knife, map. The knife was made of an offshoot brass-like alloy, not hard enough to produce a spark when struck.

She was not going to pretend she knew how to light a fire with two sticks. Steel, she needed steel. Fuck.

Mala sat inside the rift she called shelter, squeezing into place, when she felt something stabbing her hips. The flask dangled from her belt, and with the lack of space, it dug into her sides. She went to pull her flask so it would not be as uncomfortable, but when her fingers touched it, she was reminded that it was made of metal.

Mala yanked the flask out of her belt and pulled it closer to her face. Of course she didn't realize until now: she wouldn't have thought of damaging her most precious possession. Sure, it was rugged, sturdy, and would at worst get some minor scratches if she was careful, but then again, it was her first trophy she swindled off a mark when she'd begun gambling, and besides, she'd be damned if she perforated it.

She caressed the flask one last time. Its texture was rough. "Carbon steel", she thought. Hard enough to work.

Mala jumped into action to take advantage of the last direct rays of the sun she would get. In a few minutes, everything would turn into a mushy red tone, and her poor dark vision would be unable to identify any useful stones to scratch her flask with. She had to act now.

... but the girl was no geologist. She knew she needed something harder than the steel she was trying to scratch, but had no idea what to look for. To make up for her ignorance, she grabbed all rocks that looked sturdy enough. One of them had to work: her life depended on it.

The moons were starting to align in the sky. The temperature was dropping. Yes, the mooncycle had already started. Better start working through that pile of rocks.

*Scratch.* Nothing. The rock was too soft and cracked. Next.

*Scratch.* Still nothing. Too round. Next.

*Scratch*. Mala scraped the skin off her knuckles: the rock was too small. Next.

*Scratch...*

A miniature yellow star emerged from the flask. Bingo. *Scratchscratchscratchscratch.*

Mala smiled. All this scratching reminded her of gambling.

*Scratchscratchscratchscratch.*

The dry grass in the pile became incandescent, then blackened. She had done it, she had started a fire.

Mala fell back in exhaustion, hyperventilating. Her hand hurt; she was sure some blood was coming off her fingers, but she succeeded. The mooncycle was off to an excellent start.

Once she recovered her breath, Mala cuddled inside of her improvised shelter; it was uncomfortable, but she was so satisfied with herself that she didn't mind a few sharp stones up her spine. She laid face up, staring at the starry sky, a little tug in the back of her mind telling her something wrong was going on with the moons, but tiredness silenced those inconsequential thoughts.

She unpacked the clothes from her backpack and covered herself with them to heat up, but it was not enough. Damn, she wished she'd packed that little something warm.

---

Mala lay back against a pile of sharp pebbles, the blowing wind screamed in her sensitive ears, cold cut her cheeks, and the pile of burning dung next to her was not helping with this sensory hell. And yet, she was grinning.

Maybe it was because the situation reminded her of what she set off to do with this journey; to become "the bestest pioneer", as she told her mom. Maybe it was because powering through a near-death experience reminded her that she may be, after all, semi-competent at this. Either way, she felt... happy. Or at least satisfied.

The view she had of the skies above was breathtaking. Never in her life had she seen stars poking through the violet crown of the sky, such a clear view of the moons, and the last sliver of sun.

The sun peeped so little from behind Cendra, the larger moon, that it seemed the iris of a sleepy eye. An eye so sleepy, it was almost closed. So closed, it was indeed swallowed whole.

Wait, that's not supposed to happen.

The sky went black in the blink of an eye, the life of its million stars sucked out by the vacuum of the void. The moons went dark, only an ominous halo marking their presence in the firmament.

Total darkness.

Mala had no words in her vocabulary to describe what she had just seen. No one did: it was the first time in recorded history that such an alignment of the celestial bodies happened. She was speechless. Mala wondered if she'd just not paid attention before to mooncycles, but she had spent most of her last year partying while the moons were up.

Mooncycles don't do that.
